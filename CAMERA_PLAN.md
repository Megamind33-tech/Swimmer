# 3D Swimming Game Broadcast & Cinematic Camera Direction Document

## 1. Objective

Build a swimming camera system that feels like a premium televised world championship, while still being readable for gameplay. The goal is not to copy real coverage shot-for-shot, but to take the parts that make real swimming broadcasts feel authentic and combine them with game-direction rules that increase drama, clarity, and player emotion.

Real swimming coverage typically revolves around a high start/finish master camera, a lower start/finish slow-motion camera, a 50m turn camera, a pool-deck tracking camera, RF handheld deck cameras, underwater cameras at the start and turn ends, an underwater tracking camera, an overhead tracking camera, a crane/jib, a marshalling-area camera for pre-race activity, and a flash-interview camera near the athletes' exit. A simplified training version of the package is often described as race follow, slash/elevated, turn camera, and handheld. Recent championship production coverage also highlights poolside handheld cameras for walkouts and post-race emotion, underwater PTZ coverage for turns, and a full-length remote track camera along the pool. Official competition guidance also requires underwater television equipment to be remote-controlled and non-obstructive.

World Aquatics' judging guidance also confirms that elite swimming uses underwater and above-water video, plus overhead high-speed video, for starts, relay exchanges, turns, and finishes. That matters for your game because it means the most dramatic moments in swimming are also the moments the real sport already treats as visually important.

## 2. Core Directing Philosophy

Your game should separate cameras into three groups:

### A. Gameplay Cameras

These must be readable and stable. They should prioritize:

- lane order
- pacing
- player input windows
- turn timing
- finish timing

### B. Broadcast Cameras

These sell the sport. They should prioritize:

- race geography
- leader separation
- overtakes
- crowd scale
- presentation quality

### C. Emotional Cameras

These sell the athlete. They should prioritize:

- nerves before the start
- scoreboard reaction
- relief, anger, joy, frustration
- rivalry
- celebration

## 3. Golden Rules

1. **The player must always know who is winning.**
2. **Do not cut away during a critical input window unless the game is in cinematic assist mode.**
3. **Use underwater shots as punctuation, not as the main readable race view.**
4. **The closer the race, the tighter the camera may become.**
5. **The shorter the race, the fewer the cuts.**
6. **After the finish, stay with emotion before menus.**
7. **Every race should feel like an event, not a clip.**

## 4. Recommended Full Camera Package

Below is the full package for a premium version of the game.

---

### CAM 01 — Arena Establishing Wide

**Purpose:** open the event with scale and prestige

**Placement:** high above the pool, centered or slightly diagonal

**FOV suggestion:** 70–85°

**Movement:** slow crane drift or slow digital push

**Focus:** full pool, scoreboard, crowd, lighting, lane symmetry

**Use:** finals intro, championship start, post-medal outro

**Shot duration:** 2.5–4.0 sec

**Trigger:** before swimmer walkouts or after returning from menu/loading

**Direction note:** this shot says, "this is a major event."

---

### CAM 02 — Marshalling / Tunnel Camera

**Purpose:** capture pre-race tension before athletes step out

**Placement:** behind the walkout point or in corridor/tunnel

**FOV suggestion:** 45–60°

**Movement:** soft handheld or stabilized shoulder feel

**Focus:** faces, breathing, coach last words, goggles, towels, shadows

**Use:** pre-race build-up, rivalry beat, semifinal/final intros

**Shot duration:** 1.5–3.0 sec

**Trigger:** when athlete intro begins

**Direction note:** use darker lighting and tighter sound design here.

---

### CAM 03 — Hero Walkout Camera

**Purpose:** make the swimmer feel like a superstar

**Placement:** low-to-mid height, in front of swimmer, moving backward

**FOV suggestion:** 40–55°

**Movement:** smooth steadicam-style backpedal

**Focus:** face, chest, cap, flag/club branding, confident walk

**Use:** main athlete entrance

**Shot duration:** 2.5–5.0 sec

**Trigger:** athlete leaves marshalling area

**Direction note:** slight side drift makes it feel more expensive.

---

### CAM 04 — Lane Introduction Portrait

**Purpose:** athlete identity close-up during name call

**Placement:** deck height, three-quarter front angle

**FOV suggestion:** 25–40°

**Movement:** mostly static, tiny push-in

**Focus:** eyes, jaw tension, goggles adjustment, deep breath

**Use:** lane-by-lane intro

**Shot duration:** 1.0–2.0 sec per swimmer

**Trigger:** commentator/announcer intro

**Direction note:** this is where rival personality is built.

---

### CAM 05 — Block Detail / Macro Insert

**Purpose:** emphasize readiness and tension

**Placement:** very low near starting block

**FOV suggestion:** 20–35°

**Movement:** static or micro slide

**Focus:** toes on edge, fingers curling on block, water drip, calf tension

**Use:** just before "take your marks"

**Shot duration:** 0.3–0.8 sec inserts

**Trigger:** final pre-start beats

**Direction note:** never overuse; this is spice.

---

### CAM 06 — Overhead Lineup Camera

**Purpose:** give complete race geography before the start

**Placement:** high centered top-down or steep overhead

**FOV suggestion:** 60–75°

**Movement:** static or slow push-in

**Focus:** all lanes, lane numbers, symmetry, field layout

**Use:** final pre-start shot

**Shot duration:** 1.5–2.5 sec

**Trigger:** on "take your marks"

**Direction note:** perfect for showing all competitors at once.

---

### CAM 07 — Start/Finish Master Camera

**Purpose:** the main broadcast race view

**Placement:** high on the start/finish side, centered on the pool

**FOV suggestion:** 24–32°

**Movement:** mostly locked, with subtle pan/track bias

**Focus:** all lanes, leaders, race order, readable spacing

**Use:** default race camera for most live action

**Shot duration:** 2.0–6.0 sec depending on event

**Trigger:** start of race, middle coverage, final fallback view

**Direction note:** this is the safest and most important camera in the game.

A traditional swimming master setup is usually high and aligned with the start/finish line so all lanes stay visible, while a lower start/finish iso handles tighter slow-motion finish coverage.

---

### CAM 08 — Start/Finish Slow-Mo ISO

**Purpose:** isolate the finish and give replay value

**Placement:** lower than the master, still aligned with start/finish

**FOV suggestion:** 15–24°

**Movement:** static or tiny pan corrections

**Focus:** winner, wall touch, shoulder line, finish precision

**Use:** replay, dramatic finish, close result

**Shot duration:** usually replay or 0.8–1.5 sec live accent

**Trigger:** final meters or result confirmation

**Direction note:** best used at the wall, not as a main live view.

---

### CAM 09 — Turn Master Camera

**Purpose:** dramatize wall approach and turn timing

**Placement:** opposite end of the pool, aligned with the turning wall

**FOV suggestion:** 24–34°

**Movement:** small pan/tilt corrections

**Focus:** wall rush, flip timing, touch, push-off

**Use:** 100m+, medley, relay exchanges near wall

**Shot duration:** 0.8–2.0 sec live, longer in replay

**Trigger:** 6–8m before wall

**Direction note:** cut in late so the wall arrives fast.

Real broadcast plans include a dedicated 50m-turn camera plus a turn slow-motion position, and championship production has specifically highlighted underwater views for tumble turns.

---

### CAM 10 — Pool-Deck Tracking Camera

**Purpose:** provide kinetic race-follow coverage

**Placement:** on the main camera side, moving parallel to the pool

**FOV suggestion:** 35–50°

**Movement:** smooth lateral tracking

**Focus:** leader pack, side profile, overtakes, stroke cadence

**Use:** primary cinematic gameplay follow

**Shot duration:** 2.0–5.0 sec

**Trigger:** after breakout, leader change, final push

**Direction note:** this should feel like the game's signature live camera.

Broadcast venue plans explicitly include a full-length poolside tracking camera parallel to the pool, and recent championship coverage used a 35m remote track camera for live race action.

---

### CAM 11 — RF Handheld Deck A

**Purpose:** close-ups before and after the race

**Placement:** pool deck near start/finish

**FOV suggestion:** 45–60°

**Movement:** stabilized handheld

**Focus:** walkouts, prep, coach reactions, winner celebration

**Use:** intros and post-race emotion

**Shot duration:** 1.0–3.0 sec

**Trigger:** pre-start, touch, scoreboard look, celebration

---

### CAM 12 — RF Handheld Deck B

**Purpose:** support deck drama and relay focus

**Placement:** mobile on pool deck, able to hit exchange and exit positions

**FOV suggestion:** 35–55°

**Movement:** handheld or monopod feel

**Focus:** outgoing relay swimmer, teammates, rival reaction, interviews

**Use:** relay mode and emotional coverage

**Shot duration:** 1.0–2.5 sec

**Trigger:** relay exchanges, upset wins, disqualifications, interviews

Poolside handheld cameras are standard for athlete walkouts, race prep, starts, post-race reaction, relay changes, and interviews.

---

### CAM 13 — Underwater Start Camera

**Purpose:** make the dive and breakout feel elite

**Placement:** submerged at the start end

**FOV suggestion:** 45–60°

**Movement:** static or slight pan

**Focus:** entries, streamline, bubbles, underwater kicks

**Use:** start replay or very short live accent

**Shot duration:** 0.5–1.0 sec live, 1.5–2.0 sec replay

**Trigger:** on start signal

---

### CAM 14 — Underwater Turn Camera

**Purpose:** show push-off and underwater discipline after turn

**Placement:** submerged near the turn wall

**FOV suggestion:** 45–60°

**Movement:** static

**Focus:** flip, wall plant, streamline, breakout

**Use:** turn replay or very short live accent

**Shot duration:** 0.5–1.0 sec live, 1.5–2.0 sec replay

**Trigger:** immediately after wall contact

---

### CAM 15 — Underwater Rail / Tracking Camera

**Purpose:** premium signature underwater race shot

**Placement:** submerged tracking system along pool length

**FOV suggestion:** 35–50°

**Movement:** lateral or front-tracking

**Focus:** bodyline, kick, glide, underwater speed, leader separation

**Use:** starts, turns, special replay, ultra-premium finals presentation

**Shot duration:** 0.6–1.2 sec live, 2.0–3.0 sec replay

**Trigger:** breakout, key mid-race insert, world-record moment

Real swim coverage plans include underwater cameras at the start and turn ends plus an underwater tracking camera that follows the full pool, and broadcast vendors market such systems specifically for world-class swimming.

---

### CAM 16 — Overhead Tracking Camera

**Purpose:** tactical clarity and relay precision

**Placement:** above pool on overhead track

**FOV suggestion:** 55–70°

**Movement:** full-length track

**Focus:** lane geometry, exchange spacing, clean tactical view

**Use:** relay exchange, tutorial mode, major replay, special intro montage

**Shot duration:** 0.8–2.0 sec live, longer in replay

**Trigger:** relay exchange windows, false start replays, lead change replay

---

### CAM 17 — Crane / Jib Camera

**Purpose:** prestige and visual sweep

**Placement:** near start/finish or medal area

**FOV suggestion:** 60–80°

**Movement:** rising arc or descending sweep

**Focus:** starting blocks, dais, warm-up zone, celebration zone

**Use:** show openers, transitions, medal sequence

**Shot duration:** 2.0–4.0 sec

**Trigger:** event open, final touch, podium reveal

Swimming outside-broadcast guidance includes crane/jib positions for angles above the warm-up area, starting blocks, and dais.

---

### CAM 18 — Finish Compression Camera

**Purpose:** maximize tension in the final meters

**Placement:** at or just behind the finish wall, looking down the lanes

**FOV suggestion:** 14–20°

**Movement:** almost none

**Focus:** fingertips, wall, lane race, last stroke

**Use:** final 7.5–12m and touch

**Shot duration:** 0.8–1.5 sec

**Trigger:** race enters finish threshold

**Direction note:** use when the finish is close or dramatic.

---

### CAM 19 — Scoreboard Reaction Camera

**Purpose:** capture the most authentic post-race emotion

**Placement:** side-front low angle near finish

**FOV suggestion:** 30–45°

**Movement:** small push-in

**Focus:** athlete lifting head, looking to board, breathing, disbelief or joy

**Use:** immediately after touch

**Shot duration:** 1.5–3.0 sec

**Trigger:** first head-up after finish

**Direction note:** do not cut away too quickly.

---

### CAM 20 — Flash Interview / Exit Camera

**Purpose:** create a professional sports-broadcast ending beat

**Placement:** athlete exit path or mixed-zone equivalent

**FOV suggestion:** 35–50°

**Movement:** light handheld

**Focus:** winner walk-off, rival reaction, coach embrace, short interview

**Use:** major wins, finals, story moments

**Shot duration:** 2.0–5.0 sec

**Trigger:** after result lock or podium transition

Broadcast plans for swimming explicitly include a marshalling-area camera for pre-race activity and a flash-interview camera at the competitor exit.

## 5. Readability Rules During Gameplay

This is where game direction must differ from TV.

### Default Readable Race Camera

Use CAM 07 or CAM 10 as your main live gameplay view.

### Lock the Camera During Skill Windows

Do not cut during:

- reaction start input
- dive timing input
- turn timing input
- finish lunge/touch input
- relay takeover input

### Underwater Limitations

Use underwater shots only:

- right after entry
- right after push-off
- in replay
- in highlight mode
- in spectator mode

For active player control, keep underwater live inserts very short.

### Keep Race Logic Visible

When the race is tight:
- frame the leader and nearest challenger together

When the player is far behind:
- widen slightly to show the target ahead

When the player is far ahead:
- allow hero framing, but keep one rival in shot if possible

## 6. Editing Rules

### Cut Only on Clean Moments

Best cut points:

- start signal
- water entry
- breakout
- 5–7m before wall
- wall contact
- breakout after turn
- last 10m
- touch
- scoreboard glance
- celebration beat

### Avoid Cutting on Messy Motion

Avoid cutting:

- mid-breath if face is distorted
- mid-splash when readability drops
- during player timing prompts
- too often in sprint races

### Suggested Average Cut Rhythm

- **50m:** 1.0–1.8 sec average during race
- **100m:** 1.5–2.5 sec average
- **200m:** 2.5–4.0 sec average
- **relay:** event-driven around exchanges

## 8. Direction of Travel

In real competition, 50m races in a 50m pool may be held either from the usual starting end to the turn end or in the opposite direction, depending on the meet setup. For a game, though, you should standardize one hero direction for visual consistency.

### Recommendation

Standardize finals and major races as:

- broadcast left-to-right in side view
- finish wall always visually familiar
- scoreboard always in a consistent screen zone

That consistency makes the game feel like a franchise.

## 9. Event-Specific Shot Plans

### 50m Event Shot Plan

This is the shortest, cleanest, most explosive event.

**Pre-race**

- CAM 01 — 2.5 sec
- CAM 03 — 2.5 sec
- CAM 04 — 1.0 sec per highlighted rival/player
- CAM 05 — 0.5 sec insert
- CAM 06 — 1.5 sec

**Start**

- CAM 07 on "take your marks"
- cut to CAM 13 at horn for 0.5–0.8 sec
- cut to CAM 10 at breakout

**Mid race**

- mostly CAM 10
- quick return to CAM 07 if lane order gets messy

**Finish**

- cut to CAM 18 in final 8–12m
- optional CAM 08 for replay only

**After race**

- CAM 19 scoreboard reaction
- CAM 11 celebration
- CAM 12 rival reaction if story mode
- CAM 20 if final/championship

**50m directing rule**

Do not overcut. The race is too short.

---

### 100m Event Shot Plan

This is the sweet spot for swimming drama because it includes both sprint intensity and one crucial turn.

**Pre-race**

Same as 50m, but add slightly more rivalry beats.

**Length 1**

- CAM 07 at start
- CAM 13 on entry
- CAM 10 through breakout and first length

**Turn sequence**

- CAM 09 at 6–8m before wall
- CAM 14 immediately after contact for 0.5–0.9 sec
- CAM 10 or CAM 07 on resurfacing

**Length 2**

- CAM 07 or CAM 10 depending on race tightness
- CAM 18 final 10m

**After race**

- CAM 19
- CAM 11
- CAM 12
- CAM 20 if featured race

**100m directing rule**

The turn is your second "start." Treat it as a major beat.

---

### 200m Event Shot Plan

This is where pacing, stamina, and tactical storytelling matter.

**Pre-race**

- CAM 01
- CAM 02
- CAM 03
- CAM 04
- CAM 06

**First 50**

- CAM 07 at start
- CAM 13 very brief
- CAM 10 through settle-in

**First turn**

- CAM 09
- CAM 14
- return to CAM 07

**Middle race**

Alternate:

- CAM 07 for readability
- CAM 10 for leader pack
- CAM 16 occasionally for tactical clarity
- CAM 15 once only, as a premium insert

**Final 50**

- increase cut frequency slightly
- CAM 10 for acceleration
- CAM 18 final 10m
- CAM 19 reaction

**200m directing rule**

Do not make it look like a sprint the whole time. The camera rhythm should breathe early, then tighten late.

---

### Relay Event Shot Plan

Relays should feel closest to football in team energy.

**Pre-race**

- CAM 01
- CAM 03 for team captain
- CAM 12 for teammates on deck
- CAM 06 full lineup

**Start**

- CAM 07
- CAM 13 brief
- CAM 10 follow

**Exchange windows**

- CAM 16 overhead tracking
- CAM 12 outgoing swimmer nerves
- CAM 09 wall approach
- CAM 14 push-off
- immediate return to CAM 07 or CAM 10

**Final leg**

- bias to CAM 10 and CAM 18
- hold longer after touch
- prioritize team reaction

**After race**

- CAM 11 and CAM 12 for team chaos
- CAM 19 scoreboard
- CAM 17 medal/celebration if major event

Overhead high-speed systems are already important in elite swimming for relay exchange verification and false starts, so using overhead relay shots in the game is highly authentic.

## 10. Replay Package

Use replay only for meaningful moments:

- perfect dive
- best breakout
- huge overtake
- elite turn
- photo finish
- rivalry upset
- record broken
- relay exchange save
- false start/disqualification drama

### Replay Camera Priority

1. CAM 08
2. CAM 13
3. CAM 14
4. CAM 15
5. CAM 16
6. CAM 18

### Replay Speed

- 70% speed for most dramatic moments
- 40–50% for finish touches
- 30–40% only for very special championship moments

## 11. Audio-to-Camera Sync Rules

### Entrance

- crowd swell rises under CAM 03
- footsteps and breathing present under CAM 02

### Start

- mute crowd slightly before horn
- sharp horn/starter cue on CAM 07
- underwater muffling on CAM 13

### Mid-Race

- water slap and breathing should dominate CAM 10
- underwater low-pass effect for CAM 14 and CAM 15

### Finish

- heartbeat layer can build in last meters
- crowd spike on wall touch
- short silence beat before scoreboard reveal can work very well

### Post-Race

- breathing and water drip should be audible on CAM 19
- crowd re-enters as emotion lands

## Implementation Notes

This camera system works best when:
- Audio design complements camera movement (see Audio-to-Camera Sync Rules)
- UI (scoreboard, timers) matches the camera's focus area
- Replay system integrates these camera specifications
- Player input windows are protected from camera cuts
- Each event type (50m, 100m, 200m, relay) follows its specific shot plan
